Rock Cannons of Gwynedd

Discovering the unique tradition of celebration carved into the landscape of Gwynedd and Wales.

Rock Cannons

All about Rock Cannons:

A rock cannon is a method of salutation and the celebration of important social, commercial and industrial events, whether they are of local or national interest, that dates back as far as the latter years of the 18th century. It was initially believed that this method of celebration was confined to the slate quarries and metal mines of Gwynedd, but research has now shown that sites exist in other parts of North Wales. As of 2001, 235 sites have been found adding up to 6,157 holes.

Fun facts about Rock Cannons:

The earliest record of cannon in Gwynedd is found in a contemporary newspaper report (1808), which gives lengthy details of the celebration in various areas of present Gwynedd of the 50th anniversary of George III's accession to the throne. It states, under the heading of JUBILEE that 'In no part of the British Empire has this event been celebrated with greater demonstrations of rejoicing, thankfulness, and all the more estimable virtues of charity and beneficence, than in this principality'. Rock cannons were used to celebrate the marriage of Albert Edward, Prince of Wales to Princess Alexandra of Denmark in 1863 as well as the Diamond Jubilee of Queen Victoria in 1897.
